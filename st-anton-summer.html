A note for the Alowedi family

We thought of your summer when we put this together.

Three hours by car, three-and-a-half by train, and you're in one of the loveliest alpine valleys in Europe — quieter than Zermatt, warmer than Saas-Fee, and built for the way you travel: a balance of long meadow walks for the grown-ups, water and wildlife for the kids, and a village small enough that everyone gets to sleep in.

Everything below was chosen with you in mind — the lifts that take strollers, the trails that end at a swing, the river crossings that pass for an adventure, and the rainy-day plans that don't feel like consolation prizes.

A different kind of Alpine summer

Forget the lift queues. Lace up your boots.

St. Anton is famous for its winters — but its summers are quietly its best-kept secret. The same gondolas that carry skiers in February shuttle hikers and bikers from the village to high-alpine passes. The same valleys hold rushing rivers, secret waterfalls, and meadows full of grazing cattle.

This is a slower, greener, more affordable St. Anton. Family-owned guesthouses replace ski-week crowds. The Galzigbahn lift pass costs a fraction of a winter day pass. And on a clear July afternoon, you can summit Valluga at 2,811 m without seeing another soul.

It's everything Switzerland sells you on, ten minutes across the Austrian border — and at half the price.

What to do up there

Sixteen ways to fill a summer day

From lazy lake swims to a 700-metre via-ferrata wall, St. Anton's summer programme spans every appetite. Most lifts run all day on the Wanderpass, so you can pack three activities into one afternoon.

Hike

High-alpine hiking

Over 300 km of marked routes from gentle valley walks to the 3-Seen-Tour and Verwall traverses.

Adventure

Mountain biking

Arlberg Trail, Rendl flow lines, and the legendary Stanton Loop — singletrack for every skill level, with bike-friendly gondolas.

Family

Galzigbahn gondola rides

The signature spinning cabins lift you to 2,185 m in eight minutes — no effort, all the views. Strollers welcome.

Family

Swimming & alpine lakes

The free WellCom pool in the village, plus crystal-clear lakes like Verwallsee and Kartellsee for a wild dip.

Adventure

Via ferrata

The Arlberger Klettersteig is one of Austria's most spectacular — a 700-metre ridge traverse for confident climbers age 12+.

Adventure

Paragliding tandems

Launch from Gampen at 1,850 m and float for 20 minutes over the village. No experience required — kids from age 5.

Adventure

Whitewater rafting

The Rosanna and Trisanna rivers feed grade II–III rapids 20 minutes down-valley. Family rafts run from age 6.

Adventure

Canyoning

Rope down hidden gorges with certified guides — the Rosanna gorge tour is the regional classic.

Family

Alm visits & cheese making

Hike up to a working dairy alm, watch the cows come in, and taste Tirolean Bergkäse hours after it's pressed.

Hike

Verwall valley

The wildest corner of the region — a protected nature reserve with hidden lakes, waterfalls, and ibex sightings.

Family

Adventure playgrounds

The Murmli-Trail at the Gampen mid-station is a 2 km themed kids' loop with marmot stations, water games, and a giant slide.

Sport

Tennis, golf & e-bikes

Outdoor courts in the village, an 18-hole course in nearby Mieming, and rental e-bikes that flatten every climb.

Adventure

Trail running

The Arlberg Giro, Run-the-Stanton, and a maze of marked routes — every July the village hosts one of Europe's top mountain races.

Family

Wildlife spotting

Marmots, ibex, chamois, and golden eagles — a 6 a.m. ride up the Galzigbahn is the region's best-kept wildlife secret.

Sport

Horseback riding

Beginner-friendly stables in nearby Pettneu offer pony rides for kids and half-day Haflinger treks through the forest.

Family

Hut overnights

Sleep under the stars at the Konstanzer Hütte or Edmund-Graf-Hütte — fairytale stone refuges accessible by an easy day's walk.

Made for small humans

The mountain meets your kids halfway

Free children's lift passes, themed kids' trails at every gondola top station, and a tourism office that organises a free supervised activity programme every weekday in July and August.

For families with children

Twelve specifics that make a difference

St. Anton has rebuilt itself as a year-round family destination. Lifts have stroller-accessible cabins, the village is car-light, and the children's programme is taken seriously enough to be funded by the Tourismusverband itself.

1

Free children's lift pass

Kids under 6 ride all gondolas free, and the family Wanderpass gives unlimited rides for every named child.

2

Murmli-Trail at Gampen

A 2 km themed circular walk built around marmot lookouts, water-pump stations, and a 50-metre tube slide.

3

WellCom indoor pool

Free entry with the guest card. Kids' pool, slide, sauna for adults, open every day in summer including rainy ones.

4

Tirolean farm visits

Pre-bookable mornings at working alms — feed calves, watch goats milked, take a wheel of fresh butter home.

5

Free guided kids' programme

Every weekday from July to mid-September, the tourism office runs a supervised mix of nature walks, climbing, and crafts.

6

Stroller-friendly trails

The Maiensee loop, the Arlbergpass promenade, and most of the Verwall valley are pram-passable.

7

Forest-themed playgrounds

Three large play parks in the village alone — including one beside the river with a 200-metre suspension bridge.

8

Mountain carts & scooters

Three-wheeled mountain carts run from the Rendlbahn — a 5 km descent suitable from age 8.

9

Climbing wall & high-rope park

An indoor wall in the village (rainy-day saviour) plus a high-ropes course with kids' and adult lines.

10

Pony rides & petting farms

Two stables in the valley run beginner pony sessions, plus a petting farm at Maiensee for under-fives.

11

Glacier outing in Pitztal

50 minutes away — a year-round glacier with a kids' snow park even in August. A great rainy-day plan-B.

12

Mostly car-free village

Hotels run electric shuttles to lifts, and the central pedestrian zone means kids can roam free.

From your door

Three hours from Zurich, give or take.

St. Anton sits roughly 200 km east of Zurich, just over the Vorarlberg border in Austria. Whether you drive, take the Railjet, or split the journey across both, you'll be unpacking before lunch.

By car

The classic Arlberg run

2 h 50approx · ~200 km
  • A3 / A13 Zurich → Sargans → Chur → Landquart
  • A14 across the Austrian border at St. Margrethen / Hörbranz to Bludenz
  • S16 Arlberg Schnellstraße through Klösterle and the 14 km Arlberg Tunnel — toll roughly €11.50
  • Or, free of charge: the scenic Arlberg Pass (B197) over 1,793 m — open all summer
  • Single Austrian motorway vignette: ~€12 for 10 days
By train

Direct Railjet, no changes

3 h 25Zurich HB → St. Anton
  • Hourly Railjet (ÖBB / SBB) departs Zurich HB direct to St. Anton am Arlberg
  • Stops in Sargans, Buchs, Feldkirch, Bludenz, Langen — then through the Arlberg rail tunnel
  • Walk-on bike spaces (reservation recommended in July / August)
  • Family carriages with play area on most departures
  • Saver fares from CHF 39 each way if booked 30 days ahead
Mix & match

Fly + drive (or train)

4 h+door to door
  • Fly into Zurich (ZRH) from anywhere in Europe, then take the Railjet from the airport station
  • Innsbruck (INN) is closer — ~1 h 15 min by train from St. Anton — but has fewer flights
  • Memmingen / Munich works for German-speaking arrivals: 2.5–3 h drive
  • Most St. Anton hotels offer free station pickup if you book a week-long stay

The verdict

Train if you have the time and want to start the holiday as soon as you sit down. Drive if you're bringing bikes, prams, or a week's worth of luggage. Either way, St. Anton is closer than most Swiss alpine destinations — and it doesn't feel like a journey, it feels like part of the trip.

One small tip

If you're driving in July or August, leave Zurich before 8 a.m. or after 4 p.m. The Arlberg Tunnel can build a 20-minute backup at noon on Saturdays — and the pass road, while gorgeous, is slow with caravans.

Timing it right

When to come, and what to expect

Lift season runs roughly mid-June to early October. The shoulder weeks are the secret months — quieter trails, lower prices, and snow lingering on the high peaks.

June
Wildflowers

Meadows in full bloom, snowfields still capping the Valluga. Some high passes need crampons until late June. Quietest month.

July
High Season

Long days, full lift programme, kids' supervised activities run daily, mountain races and folk festivals every weekend.

August
Warm Days

Warmest swimming temperatures, busiest week is mid-August. Book hotels at least three months ahead. Long evenings on the terraces.

Sept–Oct
Larch Gold

Crisp mornings, the larches turn copper, and trails empty out completely. Last gondolas usually run to mid-October.
